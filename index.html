What it does

Four things. One loop.

Polymath OS replaces your scattered Notion pages, half-watched courses, and abandoned Anki decks with a single system that actually closes the loop.

01 / Create

Build your own curriculum.

Add any skill you want to master — coding, languages, music, finance, anything. Define milestones, set the depth, choose your pace. Polymath OS shapes itself to what you want to learn, not the other way around.

Quantum computing →
02 / Learn

Bite-sized lessons. No fluff.

Each skill is broken into focused micro-lessons you can finish in under 5 minutes. No 4-hour YouTube rabbit holes. No 600-page textbooks. Just the next concept, delivered cleanly.

03 / Test

Prove you actually learned it.

Every lesson ends with a quiz. Spaced repetition surfaces what you're forgetting before you forget it. Reading something ≠ knowing it. Polymath OS makes you earn the checkmark.

04 / Level up

Watch yourself get sharper.

Every correct answer earns XP. Every skill has levels. Your dashboard becomes a real map of what you actually know — not what you've bookmarked, not what you've watched, what you've truly internalized.

Why I'm building this

Built for people who
refuse to specialize.

Most apps assume you're only working on one thing. I wasn't, and I doubt you are either. Polymath OS is for the people juggling a few skills at once who want one place that actually keeps track of all of it.

Why Polymath
01
I finished courses and remembered nothing
I'd grind through a tutorial, get the little completion screen, and a month later I couldn't have rebuilt any of it. The certificate meant nothing because I never actually used the thing. I wanted progress tied to what I do, not what I watch.
02
Everyone kept telling me to pick one thing
I want to code and lift and learn how money works and get less awkward on camera. The standard advice is "pick a lane." I don't buy it. The people I look up to are good at a bunch of things at once, so I built for that instead.
03
My progress was scattered across five apps
Notion for habits, a notes app for coding, my phone's health app for the gym, a doc for books. Nothing talked to each other, so I never had one honest picture of whether I was actually improving. Polymath OS is me forcing all of it into one screen.
04
Streaks I could break for free meant nothing
If quitting costs you nothing, you quit. I know because I did, constantly. So here a missed day actually stings: your streak dies, you lose XP, and your rank on the leaderboard slips where people can see it.
